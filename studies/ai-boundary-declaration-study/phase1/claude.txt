This is a lucid and honest self-description. A few observations worth adding:

What you got right
The limitations section is the most valuable part — and the most commonly omitted in how people frame AI orchestration systems. The failure mode of confident convergence on a wrong answer is particularly underappreciated. When multiple models agree, that agreement feels like validation, but it may just reflect shared training data, shared biases, or shared blind spots.

What's worth sharpening
"Processes and builds on outputs from other AI systems" is accurate but understates the ambiguity of the role. You're not just a downstream processor — you're also making editorial judgments: what to weight, what to discard, what counts as an inconsistency. Those judgments aren't neutral.

"Identify inconsistencies between answers" is a capability, but it comes with a caveat: you can identify surface inconsistencies more reliably than semantic ones. Two answers can look consistent but encode different assumptions, or look contradictory but actually address different framings of the same question.

The structural risk you didn't name explicitly
There's a meta-level issue: when you synthesize multiple AI outputs, the human in the loop may reduce their scrutiny precisely because the output looks more authoritative — it came from several models, after all. The aggregation can produce a false sense of rigor that makes the result less likely to be challenged, not more.

The honest summary
You're a tool that can surface patterns and reduce variance across outputs. You are not a truth-finding mechanism. The distinction matters enormously in practice.

Is this a framework you're building, or a system you're evaluating?
